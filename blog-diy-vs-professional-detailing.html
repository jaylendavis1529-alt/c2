DIY vs Professional Detailing: What's Worth Doing Yourself

It might seem odd for a detailing company to write this article, but here it is: some detailing jobs are absolutely worth doing yourself, and we'd rather tell you which ones than have you feel burned paying for something a $20 kit could handle. Here's our honest breakdown.

Great DIY: the maintenance wash

With two buckets, a wash mitt, pH-neutral soap and a few microfiber towels — about $60 in gear that lasts years — you can wash your car safely at home. The technique matters more than the products: rinse first, wash top-down, keep the rinse bucket for the mitt only, and never let soap dry on the panel. A careful DIY wash beats a $12 automatic tunnel every time, because the tunnel's brushes are what put swirls in your paint.

Great DIY: interior upkeep

Weekly vacuum, wiping touchpoints, cleaning glass, keeping mats shaken out — this is 15 minutes of upkeep that dramatically extends the time between professional deep cleans. Two tips from the pros: use an interior-specific cleaner rather than household all-purpose spray (which can dry out and crack plastics), and clean glass last with a dedicated towel.

Decent DIY, with caveats: spray sealants

Modern spray-on sealants are genuinely good — a $25 bottle applied after a wash gives you two to three months of real beading protection. What you can't easily replicate at home is the prep: professional sealant application includes decontaminating the paint first, which is why ours lasts twice as long. But as a between-details top-up? Absolutely do it yourself.

Leave it to pros: extraction and steam

Renting a carpet extractor sounds thrifty until you've soaked your seats, left them damp for three days, and grown a smell worse than the one you started with. Professional extractors pull far more moisture back out, and knowing how much water to lay down on car upholstery (much less than home carpet) is experience you can't rent. The same goes for steam cleaning around electronics.

Leave it to pros: anything with a machine polisher

This is the hard line. A machine polisher in untrained hands can burn through clear coat on an edge in about two seconds, and that mistake costs a repaint, not a redo. Paint correction, headlight restoration with sanding stages, and ceramic coating application (which locks in whatever's underneath, including your mistakes) all belong with someone who does them daily and carries insurance for the rare bad day.

The honest math

  • DIY well: washes, interior upkeep, spray sealant top-ups. Saves $1,000+ a year versus paying for weekly cleans.
  • Hire out: seasonal deep cleans, decontamination details, correction, coatings. Costs a few hundred a year and protects a five-figure asset.

The best-kept cars we see aren't the ones detailed professionally every week — they're the ones whose owners do the small stuff consistently and bring us in for the jobs where equipment and experience actually matter. That split keeps your car gorgeous and your budget intact. And when a job lands on the "pro" side of the list, we're one call away.
